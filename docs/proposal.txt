       VS-LeakKG: A Contamination Knowledge Graph for
          Leakage-Controlled Virtual Screening Benchmarks

                                             Anonymous Authors

                                                                Abstract
            Structure-based virtual screening has increasingly been formulated as a protein­ligand re-
        trieval problem, where protein pockets and candidate ligands are embedded into a shared repre-
        sentation space and ranked by similarity. Despite strong reported performance on benchmarks
        such as DUD-E, DEKOIS, and LIT-PCBA, the reliability of these results remains difficult to
        assess due to potential train­test contamination and benchmark-specific shortcuts. Existing
        evaluation protocols often remove exact target or ligand overlaps, but they rarely account for
        indirect leakage through ligand scaffolds, homologous proteins, structurally similar pockets,
        reused assays, shared publications, source-specific decoy protocols, or temporal overlap.
            We propose VS-LeakKG, a contamination-aware heterogeneous graph framework for au-
        diting, constructing, and retraining on leakage-controlled virtual screening benchmarks. VS-
        LeakKG represents benchmark examples, proteins, pockets, ligands, scaffolds, assays, publi-
        cations, dataset sources, decoy protocols, and timestamps as typed nodes, with typed edges
        encoding possible leakage pathways. The graph enables example-level contamination scoring,
        multi-axis cold split generation, shortcut diagnostics, and clean train/validation/test construc-
        tion. We further define a retraining protocol in which modern protein­ligand retrieval models
        are trained and selected only on leakage-controlled partitions. Our framework therefore pro-
        vides a benchmark-governance layer for virtual screening, allowing models to be evaluated under
        progressively stricter and reproducible generalization regimes.

1 Introduction

Structure-based virtual screening (SBVS) is a central task in early-stage drug discovery, aiming
to identify active ligands for a target protein from a large candidate library. Recent contrastive
protein­ligand models have reformulated SBVS as a retrieval problem, where protein pockets and
ligands are embedded into a shared representation space and ranked by similarity. This paradigm
enables efficient large-scale screening and has led to strong reported results on established bench-
marks such as DUD-E, DEKOIS, and LIT-PCBA.

    However, the reliability of these reported gains remains difficult to assess. Many widely used vir-
tual screening benchmarks contain hidden shortcuts that can inflate performance without requiring
models to learn true protein­ligand interactions. For instance, DUD-E-style benchmarks construct
property-matched but topologically dissimilar decoys, allowing ligand-only models to distinguish
actives from decoys by exploiting chemical artifacts rather than binding mechanisms. In addition,
modern protein­ligand models are often trained or pretrained on large heterogeneous resources
such as ChEMBL, BindingDB, PDBBind, and PubChem-derived datasets. These sources overlap
with downstream benchmarks through multiple indirect channels, including shared ligands, similar
scaffolds, homologous proteins, structurally similar pockets, reused assays, common publications,
and source-specific decoy generation protocols. As a result, a benchmark example may appear
"unseen" at the level of an exact protein­ligand pair while still being highly contaminated through
its ligand scaffold, protein family, binding pocket, or assay provenance.

                                                                1
    Existing efforts address important parts of this problem, but none fully captures the multi-axis
contamination structure of virtual screening. DrugOOD studies out-of-distribution generalization
for drug­target affinity prediction using domain annotations such as assay, scaffold, protein, and
protein family. DataSAIL provides a general-purpose framework for leakage-reduced splitting in
biological datasets by minimizing similarity across partitions. Recent leakage-aware protein­ligand
benchmarks such as LP-PDBBind and CleanSplit focus on binding affinity or scoring-function
evaluation. BayesBind moves closer to SBVS by constructing a benchmark with targets structurally
dissimilar from the training set. These works demonstrate the importance of rigorous evaluation,
but they do not explicitly model virtual-screening contamination as a typed, high-order relational
structure across proteins, pockets, ligands, scaffolds, assays, sources, decoy protocols, and time.

    We argue that virtual screening requires a benchmark-governance framework rather than an-
other single benchmark split. In particular, train­test contamination should be treated as a het-
erogeneous graph problem: examples are connected through biological, chemical, structural, ex-
perimental, and provenance relations, and leakage may occur through short paths in this graph.
Such a formulation enables three complementary capabilities. First, it enables dataset auditing
through example-level and axis-specific contamination scores. Second, it enables construction of
train/validation/test splits that explicitly forbid leakage paths across predefined axes. Third, it
enables controlled retraining of modern protein­ligand retrieval models under the same leakage-
controlled protocol, so that performance reflects generalization rather than overlap with the training
data or validation leakage during model selection.

    To this end, we propose VS-LeakKG, a contamination-aware heterogeneous graph framework
for auditing and constructing leakage-controlled virtual screening benchmarks. Unlike biomedical
knowledge graphs designed for link prediction or drug repurposing, VS-LeakKG is designed for
benchmark integrity: its nodes and edges encode potential leakage pathways between training,
validation, and test examples. Using this graph, we quantify contamination along ligand, scaffold,
protein, pocket, assay, source, and temporal axes; generate clean multi-axis train/validation/test
splits; retrain representative SBVS models on the resulting partitions; and evaluate whether current
methods continue to outperform shortcut baselines when contamination is controlled.

2 Contributions

Our main contributions are as follows.

· A contamination knowledge graph for virtual screening. We introduce VS-LeakKG, a
   typed heterogeneous graph that connects benchmark examples to proteins, protein families, bind-
   ing pockets, pocket clusters, ligands, molecular scaffolds, assays, publications, dataset sources,
   decoy protocols, and timestamps. Unlike conventional biomedical knowledge graphs that are pri-
   marily used for prediction, VS-LeakKG is constructed to expose potential train­validation­test
   leakage pathways in virtual screening benchmarks.

· Example-level and axis-specific contamination scoring. We define a path-based contam-
   ination score that measures how strongly an example is connected to a reference split through
   high-confidence leakage paths. The score is decomposed into ligand-level, scaffold-level, protein-
   level, pocket-level, assay-level, source-level, and temporal components, allowing us to identify
   not only whether a benchmark is contaminated, but also which type of shortcut dominates.

· Leakage-controlled train/validation/test split construction. We propose a graph-based
   split construction procedure that first collapses examples connected by forbidden leakage paths

                                                                2
   into leakage groups, and then partitions these groups into train, validation, and test sets. This
   ensures that validation and test examples are not connected to the training set through explicitly
   defined ligand, scaffold, protein, pocket, assay, source, or temporal leakage axes. The resulting
   splits include ligand-clean, scaffold-clean, protein-clean, pocket-clean, dual-clean, and strict-
   clean regimes.

· Model retraining under clean splits. We define a retraining protocol for modern protein­
   ligand retrieval models in which model fitting, hyperparameter selection, and final testing are
   all performed on VS-LeakKG-clean partitions. This protocol distinguishes leakage in training
   data from leakage in validation-based checkpoint selection, and allows fair comparison between
   models under the same cold-start assumptions.

· Shortcut diagnostics for benchmark validity. We introduce diagnostic baselines that re-
   quire little or no protein­ligand interaction modeling, including ligand-only predictors, source-
   only predictors, contamination-nearest-neighbor predictors, and dummy-receptor controls. These
   diagnostics test whether high virtual screening performance can be achieved by exploiting chem-
   ical, source, or relational artifacts rather than learning structure-based binding signals.

· A systematic re-evaluation of modern protein­ligand retrieval models. We apply
   VS-LeakKG to audit established and modern virtual screening benchmarks, including DUD-E,
   LIT-PCBA, and BayesBind-style settings, and re-evaluate representative retrieval-based models
   such as DrugCLIP, S2Drug, LigUnity, HypSeek, and ConGLUDe. We report standard metrics
   such as AUROC, BEDROC, and enrichment factors, as well as contamination-aware metrics such
   as performance by contamination decile, worst-group performance, clean-split performance, and
   the gap between interaction-based models and shortcut baselines.

· Evidence that benchmark conclusions depend on contamination control. Through
   contamination-aware evaluation and clean-split retraining, we test whether model rankings and
   absolute performance change when ligand, scaffold, protein, pocket, assay, source, and temporal
   leakage are controlled simultaneously. These findings motivate measuring progress in virtual
   screening with benchmark-integrity diagnostics rather than only aggregate enrichment metrics
   on legacy decoy-based benchmarks.

3 Method

3.1 Problem Formulation

Let a virtual screening corpus consist of a set of examples

                                D = {xi}Ni=1.

Each example is represented as

                                xi = (pi, bi, i, yi, si),

where pi denotes a target protein, bi denotes a binding pocket or predicted pocket, i denotes
a ligand, yi  {0, 1} denotes the active/inactive label, and si denotes metadata such as assay,
publication, dataset source, timestamp, and decoy protocol.

    A conventional benchmark split partitions the corpus into training, validation, and test subsets:

                                D = Dtrain  Dval  Dtest.

                                3
However, this split may still be contaminated if validation or test examples are connected to the
training set through ligand similarity, scaffold identity, protein homology, pocket similarity, assay
reuse, shared publications, source-specific decoy protocols, or temporal overlap. Our goal is twofold:
(i) to quantify this contamination in existing benchmarks, and (ii) to construct leakage-controlled
train/validation/test splits for retraining and evaluating virtual screening models.

3.2 Constructing the Contamination Knowledge Graph

We construct a typed heterogeneous graph

                                                       G = (V, E, R),

where V denotes nodes, E denotes edges, and R denotes edge types. The graph contains multiple
node types relevant to virtual screening contamination.

    Edges encode possible leakage pathways. For example, an example_has_ligand edge con-
nects an example to its ligand, a ligand_has_scaffold edge connects a ligand to its scaffold,
and a protein_in_cluster edge connects a protein to a sequence-identity cluster. Additional
edges encode ligand Tanimoto similarity, pocket similarity, common assay membership, common
publication, common data source, and shared decoy protocol.

    Each edge type r  R is assigned a leakage weight wr  [0, 1], where larger values indicate
stronger evidence of potential contamination. For instance, exact ligand identity receives a larger
weight than scaffold similarity, and exact protein identity receives a larger weight than family-level
similarity.

This decomposition allows us to diagnose which form of contamination dominates each benchmark
or split.

3.4 Forbidden Leakage Relations

To construct clean train/validation/test partitions, we define a set of forbidden leakage relations
F  R. These are relations that are not allowed to connect examples across train, validation, and
test partitions. The strictest setting includes exact ligand identity, scaffold identity, high ligand
similarity, exact protein identity, sequence-cluster membership, pocket-cluster membership, assay
overlap, publication overlap, source-specific decoy protocol overlap, and invalid temporal ordering.

    The choice of F defines the evaluation regime. A scaffold-clean split forbids scaffold relations;
a protein-clean split forbids protein-cluster relations; a strict-clean split forbids all high-confidence
relations in Table 2.

3.5 Leakage Group Construction

Given the full graph G and the forbidden relation set F, we construct a forbidden subgraph
                                     GF = (V, EF ), EF = {e  E : r(e)  F }.

We then compute connected components over benchmark examples induced by GF . Each connected
component defines a leakage group

                 gm = {xi : xi is connected to other examples through forbidden paths}.

                                                                5
All examples in the same leakage group must be assigned to the same partition. This prevents
a scaffold, protein cluster, pocket cluster, assay, or source-specific group from being split across
training and testing.

    This group-level construction is the key step that converts graph contamination into a practical
clean split. Rather than checking only pairwise exact duplicates after splitting, VS-LeakKG pre-
vents leakage before model training by ensuring that entire leakage groups are assigned atomically.

where D+ denotes active examples. Additional constraints enforce minimum numbers of targets,
ligands, active compounds, inactive compounds, and screening candidates in validation and test.

    In practice, we use a two-stage procedure. First, leakage groups are constructed by connected
components under F. Second, groups are assigned to train/validation/test using a greedy or
integer-programming-based heuristic that preserves the desired split ratio while minimizing residual
cross-partition contamination. We explicitly report residual contamination after splitting, rather
than claiming that the split is leakage-free in an absolute sense.

    The resulting splits are released under multiple regimes:

· Ligand-clean: exact ligand overlap is forbidden across partitions.

· Scaffold-clean: scaffold overlap is forbidden across partitions.

· Protein-clean: proteins from the same sequence cluster are assigned to the same partition.

· Pocket-clean: similar pocket clusters are assigned to the same partition.

· Assay-clean: examples from the same assay or publication are assigned to the same partition.

· Dual-clean: both ligand/scaffold and protein/pocket contamination are controlled.

· Strict-clean: ligand, scaffold, protein, pocket, assay, source, protocol, and temporal contami-
   nation are controlled simultaneously.

3.7 Clean-Split Model Retraining Protocol

After constructing VS-LeakKG-clean partitions, we retrain protein­ligand retrieval models using
the same split for all methods. For a model f, training is performed only on Dtrain:

                                                = arg min L(f; Dtrain).

                                                                                    

Hyperparameters and checkpoints are selected using only the leakage-controlled validation split:

                                                ^ = arg max M (f; Dval),

                                                                                 

where M is a validation metric such as BEDROC or EF1%. Final results are reported once on Dtest:

                                                   Score = M (f^; Dtest).
This protocol prevents leakage not only in final testing, but also in model selection. We therefore
distinguish two failure modes: training contamination, where test-like examples are present in
the training set, and validation contamination, where checkpoint selection is biased by validation
examples that are too close to training or test examples.

    For models that use both structure-based and ligand-based data, such as unified protein­ligand
retrieval models, both data streams are filtered through the same VS-LeakKG split. Structure-
based complexes are assigned according to their protein, pocket, and ligand groups; ligand-based
assay records are assigned according to assay, ligand, scaffold, protein, publication, and temporal
groups. This ensures that pretraining and fine-tuning data do not leak into validation or test
through different data modalities.

3.8 Shortcut Diagnostic Baselines

A key use of VS-LeakKG is to test whether benchmark performance can be achieved without
modeling protein­ligand interactions. We introduce four diagnostic baselines.

Ligand-only baseline. This baseline uses only ligand fingerprints, scaffold identity, or physic-
ochemical descriptors to classify active and inactive compounds. Strong performance from this
baseline indicates that the benchmark contains ligand-side or decoy-selection artifacts.

Source-only baseline. This baseline uses only dataset source, assay type, publication, or decoy
protocol. Strong performance indicates source-specific label artifacts.

Contamination nearest-neighbor baseline. For a test example xt, this baseline finds the most
contaminated training example:

                                              x = arg max C(xt, {xi}),

                                                                               xi Dtrain

and transfers its label or ranking score to xt. If this baseline performs competitively with deep
protein­ligand models, then relational leakage alone may explain a substantial fraction of bench-
mark performance.

Dummy-receptor baseline. This baseline replaces the true protein or pocket with a fixed
dummy receptor representation while preserving ligand information. Strong performance indicates
that the model does not rely on target-specific structural information.

3.9 Evaluation Protocol

We evaluate models under both legacy and clean-split regimes. Given a query protein or pocket,
each model ranks candidate ligands by a predicted score. We report standard virtual screening
metrics:

                               AUROC, BEDROC, EF0.5%, EF1%, EF5%.
In addition, we report contamination-aware metrics:

· contamination distribution before and after clean split construction;

· performance by contamination decile;

· worst-group performance over scaffold, protein-family, pocket-cluster, assay, and source groups;

· model ranking changes from legacy evaluation to strict-clean retraining;

· relative gap between interaction-based models and shortcut baselines;

· validation-leakage effect, measured by comparing checkpoint selection on leaked versus clean
   validation splits.

    This evaluation distinguishes four possible outcomes. First, if a model performs well only in
high-contamination regimes, its apparent success may be driven by memorization or shortcut learn-
ing. Second, if ligand-only or source-only baselines remain competitive, the benchmark may not
require structure-based interaction modeling. Third, if a model performs well on legacy evaluation
but drops substantially after clean-split retraining, then the legacy benchmark likely overestimates
generalization. Fourth, if a model maintains strong performance under strict-clean splits and con-
sistently outperforms shortcut baselines, this provides stronger evidence of genuine protein­ligand
generalization.

3.10 Optional: Contamination-Aware Training

Although the primary goal of VS-LeakKG is benchmark governance and clean evaluation, the
graph can also guide model training. We define contamination-aware contrastive learning by select-
ing hard negatives using graph relations. For a positive protein­ligand pair (bi, i), hard negatives
are selected from ligands that are chemically similar but experimentally inactive, or from ligands
connected through scaffolds but not known to bind the same pocket.

    A general training objective can be written as

                         L = Lcontrastive + 1Lsource-adv + 2LgroupDRO + 3LKG-hard,

where Lsource-adv discourages the representation from encoding dataset-source shortcuts, LgroupDRO
improves worst-group performance across scaffold, protein, pocket, and assay groups, and LKG-hard
encourages discrimination among graph-selected hard negatives. This extension is optional; the
core contribution of VS-LeakKG remains the contamination-aware benchmark construction and
clean-split retraining framework.

4 Discussion

The proposed framework differs from existing virtual screening benchmarks in both goal and rep-
resentation. DUD-E, DEKOIS, LIT-PCBA, and BayesBind provide specific benchmark datasets,
while VS-LeakKG provides a general mechanism for auditing, constructing, and retraining on
benchmark splits. DrugOOD and DataSAIL address out-of-distribution evaluation and leakage-
reduced splitting in broader biological machine learning settings, but they do not explicitly model
the typed contamination pathways that arise in structure-based virtual screening. Biomedical
knowledge graphs such as Hetionet and PrimeKG integrate biological knowledge for prediction and
reasoning, whereas VS-LeakKG uses a heterogeneous graph as a benchmark-integrity object.

    This distinction is important because virtual screening contamination is inherently multi-relational.
A benchmark can be clean along one axis while contaminated along another. For example, a test
protein may be absent from training but belong to a highly similar pocket cluster; a test ligand
may be unique but share a scaffold with many training ligands; or a target-fishing experiment
may be held out as a dataset while many of its ligands already appear in ChEMBL. By exposing
these paths explicitly, VS-LeakKG enables more transparent and reliable assessment of progress
in virtual screening.

    The clean-split retraining protocol is equally important. Auditing existing benchmarks can re-
veal potential contamination, but retraining under graph-controlled partitions tests whether models
continue to generalize once those pathways are removed. This turns VS-LeakKG from a diagnostic
tool into a reusable benchmark construction framework. The resulting evaluation does not claim
to remove all possible sources of leakage; instead, it makes the controlled axes explicit, quantifies
residual contamination, and provides a reproducible basis for comparing virtual screening models
under well-defined cold-start assumptions.

5 Conclusion

We presented VS-LeakKG, a contamination-aware heterogeneous graph framework for reliable
virtual screening evaluation and clean benchmark construction. The framework models train­
validation­test contamination as typed paths across ligands, scaffolds, proteins, pockets, assays,
sources, decoy protocols, and time. It supports example-level contamination scoring, leakage-
group construction, multi-axis train/validation/test splitting, shortcut diagnostics, and clean-split
retraining of modern protein­ligand retrieval models. By shifting the focus from single benchmark
scores to benchmark integrity and leakage-controlled retraining, VS-LeakKG aims to provide a
more reliable basis for measuring progress in structure-based virtual screening.
